PROGRAM PHILOSOPHY

Quality over quantity. Breath and control over speed. We prioritize full range of motion, scapular and hip mobility, posterior chain strength, and tension release. This is not about chasing reps — it’s about feeling strong, stable, and fluid in your body again.

THE FOUNDATION

Why this plan works for you

You’ve already done the hardest part: getting sober and deciding to rebuild. Your body remembers how to move well — we’re simply waking it back up with intelligent, progressive calisthenics that respect where you are today (stiff, tense, posture affected) while steadily expanding your capacity.

This program emphasizes:

  • Full range of motion in every rep — the fastest path back to flexibility and joint health.
  • Posterior chain & scapular work to counteract forward posture and tension.
  • Unilateral movements (single-leg, single-arm patterns) to fix imbalances and improve real-world function and balance.
  • Daily mobility as non-negotiable “movement hygiene” to keep stiffness at bay even on non-workout days.
  • Breath + nervous system — longer exhales in cool-downs and mobility to down-regulate tension you carry.

You can train 3 or 4 times per week. Sessions are deliberately kept to 30–45 minutes so they’re sustainable and don’t become another source of stress. Everything is bodyweight (with simple household options). No gym required. Perfect for private home practice.

Baseline Assessment

Do these simple tests before you begin (and again every 4 weeks). They give you objective feedback and motivation as stiffness decreases and strength returns.

Strength & Endurance
  1. Max good-form push-ups (knee, incline, or full — note which)
  2. Max plank hold time (forearm or high plank)
  3. Superman or prone Y-raise hold time (seconds)
  4. Bodyweight squat reps or depth achieved (full ROM? heels down?)
Mobility & Posture
  1. Forward fold: fingertips to floor? How many inches from toes?
  2. Wall angel test: can arms slide up/down wall without shrug or arch?
  3. Single-leg balance: time eyes open, then closed (each leg)
  4. Subjective stiffness (1–10) morning vs evening + posture photo (side view)

Record in a notebook or simple spreadsheet. Retest every 4 weeks. Celebrate improvements in how you feel moving through daily life even more than the numbers.

NON-NEGOTIABLE

Daily Mobility Routine (10–15 min every day)

Do this even on workout days — it’s your movement hygiene

Stiffness and tension accumulate from inactivity, stress, and old movement patterns. This short daily practice is the single highest-ROI thing you can do. It lubricates joints, resets posture, down-regulates the nervous system, and makes the strength work more effective and safer.

1
Breath & Nervous System Reset 2 min

Lie on your back (or seated). Place one hand on belly, one on chest. Breathe so belly rises more than chest. Make exhales longer than inhales (e.g. inhale 4, exhale 6–8). This activates parasympathetic system and begins releasing stored tension.

2
Cat-Cow + Child’s Pose Flow 2 min

On all fours. Inhale: drop belly, lift chest and tailbone (Cow). Exhale: round spine, tuck chin and tail (Cat). Move slowly. After 8–10 cycles, sit hips back to Child’s Pose, arms long, and gently rock side to side. Then thread the needle (one arm under body) 5× each side.

3
World’s Greatest Stretch 1 min/side

From lunge position (back knee down or up). Front foot outside hand. Rotate torso toward front leg and reach top arm to ceiling. Then drop elbow to inside of front foot and reach opposite arm forward (hamstring + thoracic). 5 slow reps per side, then hold the deepest position 20–30s.

4
Hip Openers (90/90 or Pigeon) 60–90s/side

90/90 stretch (front shin parallel to body, back leg behind) or classic Pigeon. Sit tall first, then fold forward gently. Add gentle rocks or rotations. These target external rotators and hip flexors — major contributors to tight posture and limited movement.

5
Thoracic Spine & Shoulder Mobility 2 min

Open-book stretch (side-lying, top knee on floor or block, rotate top arm open like a book) 8–10 slow reps/side. Or prone cobra / thoracic extensions over a rolled towel under mid-back. Follow with wall slides or floor “snow angels” 8–10 reps, focusing on keeping low back down and arms long.

6
Lower Body & Finishing Flow 2–3 min

Kneeling hip flexor stretch with gentle reach overhead (30–45s/side). Downward Dog — pedal the feet, bend knees generously, then lift one leg high and open hip. Finish with gentle neck circles or chin tucks and a full-body shake-out or 10 slow squats to integrate.

Pro tip: Do this routine first thing in the morning or right before bed. Many people notice reduced morning stiffness within 7–10 days. If a particular area feels especially tight, spend extra 30–60 seconds there.
THE CORE PRACTICE

The Main Workout (3–4× per week • 30–45 min)

Perform as a circuit: move through the 5 exercises with minimal rest between them (20–40 seconds to transition and catch breath). Complete 3–4 full rounds. Rest 60–90 seconds between rounds. Total work time ~25 minutes. Focus on control and full range — never sacrifice form for speed or reps.

5-Minute Dynamic Warm-Up (do before every workout)

• Arm circles (forward & back, big + small) — 20 total
• Leg swings (front/back + lateral) — 10 each direction/leg
• Hip circles + gentle torso twists — 8 each way
• Cat-Cow — 8 slow reps
• World’s Greatest Stretch (dynamic) — 4 per side
• Glute bridges or hip thrusts — 10–12 (activate posterior chain)
• March in place or light step-touches with arm swings — 45–60s
Purpose: raise core temp, lubricate joints, activate glutes & scapular stabilizers, prepare nervous system.
🦵

Squat Pattern

3 sets × 10–15 reps (or 30–45s hold)
Lower body • Hips • Ankles

Form Cues

  • • Feet ~shoulder-width or slightly wider, toes turned out 5–15°
  • • Sit back and down (like sitting into a chair behind you)
  • • Knees track over 2nd–3rd toe (don’t cave in)
  • • Chest stays proud, core braced, weight in mid-foot/heels
  • • Go as deep as you can with good form and heels down — pause 1–2s at bottom
  • • Drive through heels to stand, squeeze glutes at top

REGRESSIONS (start here if needed)

Hold onto chair/back of couch for support • Box squat (sit to chair) • Partial ROM • Slow tempo only

PROGRESSIONS

Add 2s pause at bottom • Jump squats • Assisted pistol squat (holding support) • Full pistol • Tempo 3-1-3
Why it helps you: Deep squat mobility directly attacks hip and ankle stiffness. Builds functional leg strength for daily life (getting up, stairs, bending).
💪

Push-Up Pattern

3 sets × 6–12 reps
Chest • Shoulders • Core

Form Cues

  • • Hands slightly wider than shoulders, fingers forward or slightly out
  • • Body in straight line (head to heels or knees). No sagging or piking
  • • Lower until chest nearly touches floor (or as low as form allows)
  • • Elbows ~45° from torso (not flared straight out)
  • • At top: protract scapula (push floor away, shoulders round slightly)
  • • At bottom: retract scapula. Brace core & glutes the entire time

REGRESSIONS

Hands elevated on couch/chair (incline) • Knee push-ups • Wall push-ups (very beginner)

PROGRESSIONS

Full push-up • Diamond (hands close) • Slow eccentric 4-sec lowering • Archer push-up • Pseudo planche lean
Why it helps you: Teaches scapular control and full shoulder range. Counteracts rounded shoulders when paired with pulling work. Builds pressing strength for daily pushing tasks.
🧍

Pull / Row Pattern

3 sets × 8–12 reps or 20–40s hold
Back • Rear shoulders • Posture

Form Cues & Options

OPTION A — With pull-up bar or sturdy table:
Inverted rows (Australian pull-ups). Body straight, pull chest toward bar/table edge, squeeze shoulder blades hard at top, control the descent. Or use resistance band for assisted pull-ups / negatives.
OPTION B — No equipment (floor):
Prone Superman or Y/T/W raises. Lie face down, arms in Y or T position. Lift chest + arms + legs simultaneously. Squeeze upper back and glutes. Hold or do slow pulses. Keep neck neutral.

REGRESSIONS

Higher bar or table (easier angle) • Superman holds only • Towel rows in doorway (face pulls style)

PROGRESSIONS

Lower bar angle • Add pause at top • Pull-up negatives (jump or step up, lower slow 4–6s) • Full pull-ups • Weighted rows
Why it helps you: Directly strengthens the muscles that pull shoulders back — the antidote to forward posture and rounded upper back. Essential for balanced shoulder health and “tall” posture.
🦶

Reverse Lunge / Split Squat

3 sets × 6–10 reps per leg
Unilateral • Hips • Balance

Form Cues

  • • Step backward into lunge. Front knee stays over ankle
  • • Lower back knee toward floor (or lightly touch for extra hip stretch)
  • • Torso stays upright, core braced, gaze forward
  • • Push through front heel to return to start
  • Bulgarian variation (advanced): Rear foot elevated on chair/couch for deeper hip flexor stretch and balance challenge

REGRESSIONS

Shorter step • Hold wall or chair for balance • Static split squat holds instead of reps

PROGRESSIONS

Walking lunges • Jump switch lunges • Bulgarian split squat • Add slow eccentric or pause at bottom • Deficit lunges
Why it helps you: Unilateral work exposes and fixes left/right imbalances. The deep hip stretch under load is gold for tight hip flexors and anterior pelvic tilt. Improves real-life balance and stability.
🧘

Core Stability (Dead Bug + Plank)

3 sets × 8–12/side (Dead Bug) or 20–60s (Plank)
Core • Posture • Control

Form Cues

Dead Bug: Lie on back, arms straight up, knees bent 90°. Slowly extend opposite arm + leg while pressing low back into floor. Exhale as you extend. Don’t let back arch. Alternate sides with control.
Bird Dog (great alternative/add-on): On all fours, extend opposite arm and leg. Keep hips square, no twisting or sagging. Hold 3–5 seconds. Excellent for posture and scapular stability.
Plank: Forearm or high plank. Body straight line. Squeeze glutes and quads. Don’t let hips sag or pike up. Breathe steadily.

REGRESSIONS

Dead Bug with knees bent or one limb at a time • Knee plank • Short hold times • Bird Dog from knees

PROGRESSIONS

Add resistance band around feet for Dead Bug • Longer holds • Side plank • Dead Bug with weight in hands • Hollow body hold
Why it helps you: Builds deep core strength and motor control without spinal loading. Directly improves posture and stability in standing and moving. The anti-extension work counters the tendency to arch or collapse under fatigue.
Circuit Flow Reminder: Squat → Push-Up → Row/Superman → Lunge/Split Squat → Core. Minimal rest between exercises. 60–90s rest after each full round. Do 3 rounds to start; add a 4th when it feels manageable. Quality always beats rushing.

Cool-Down & Static Stretching (5–8 min)

Never skip this. Static stretching + longer exhales is where you “teach” your nervous system to release chronic tension. Hold each 20–40 seconds, 1–3 rounds. Breathe slowly and deeply — exhale longer than inhale.

Child’s Pose / Puppy Pose
Hips back, arms long or one arm threaded. Great for lats, shoulders, mid-back release.
Downward Dog + Pedal
Hamstrings, calves, shoulders. Bend knees as much as needed. Alternate lifting heels.
Pigeon or Figure-4
Deep external rotator and glute stretch. Sit tall first, then fold. Common tight spot for many.
Low Lunge + Hip Flexor Reach
Kneeling lunge, back knee down. Gently reach arm overhead and lean slightly away. Crucial for posture.
Seated Forward Fold
Hamstrings + lower back. Hinge from hips, slight knee bend ok. Use strap or towel if needed.
Doorway Chest Opener + Quad Stretch
Open pecs and anterior shoulders. Standing quad stretch (or lying). Pairs perfectly with pulling work.
Optional SMR (Self-Myofascial Release): If you have a foam roller or ball: upper/mid back (extension), lats/side, quads, glutes, calves. Slow rolls, 30–90s per area. Avoid rolling directly on joints or low back.

Progression Guidelines

Weeks 1–2: Prioritize form, breathing, and showing up. Use easier regressions. Note how your body feels before/after.
Weeks 3–6: Increase reps, hold times, or depth. Try the next progression on 1–2 exercises per session when form is solid. Add a 4th round when 3 feels easy.
Every 4 weeks: Retest your baseline assessments. Take side-view posture photos. Journal subjective changes (easier to bend over? Less morning stiffness? Stand taller longer?).
Deload weeks: Every 6–8 weeks, reduce volume or intensity ~40–50% (fewer rounds, easier variations, shorter holds). This prevents burnout and allows adaptation.

Tracking Your Progress

Simple is best. Use a notebook, Google Sheet, or app like Hevy or Strong.

  • Each session: Date, which workout, reps/holds achieved, how you felt (energy 1–10, stiffness 1–10 before & after), any notes on form or tight spots.
  • Weekly: Average stiffness, how daily movements feel (e.g. “putting on socks easier”, “less tension in shoulders while driving”).
  • Monthly: Retest baselines + progress photos + one paragraph reflection.

Non-scale victories matter most here: better posture in the mirror, less “creaky” feeling, more confidence moving, deeper sleep, steadier mood.

Recovery, Nutrition & Supporting Your Journey

Sleep & Stress

7.5–9 hours consistent sleep is non-negotiable for tissue repair, nervous system recovery, and mood. The mobility + breath work you’re doing helps down-regulate tension. Exercise supports sobriety by providing routine, dopamine, and a healthy focus — pair it with whatever else helps you stay grounded.

Nutrition Basics

Aim for ~1.6–2.2 g protein per kg body weight (~145–200 g/day) spread across meals to support muscle repair. Include complex carbs around workouts and plenty of vegetables/fruits for micronutrients and recovery. Stay hydrated (3.5–4.5 L water/day). Omega-3s (fish or supplement) and magnesium can help with inflammation and relaxation. This isn’t about perfection — consistent good-enough eating supports consistent training.

Movement as Self-Care

Rebuilding fitness after a period of lower activity is an act of self-respect. Some days will feel harder than others — that’s normal. On low-energy or high-tension days, it’s okay (and wise) to do only the daily mobility or a lighter 2-round version. The goal is consistency over years, not intensity over weeks. Your body and mind will thank you.

Safety, Listening to Your Body & Professional Guidance

This plan is general guidance based on the information you provided (no current physical limitations, beginner-to-intermediate experience, goals centered on function and mobility). It is not medical advice. Before starting — especially after a period of reduced activity and during/after sobriety transition — consider checking in with a physician or physical therapist.

Stop and regress or rest if you experience: Sharp joint pain (vs. normal muscle fatigue/burn), dizziness, chest pain, sudden swelling, or pain that worsens with movement. Muscle soreness (DOMS) 24–48h later is normal and usually helped by light movement and the daily mobility.

Breathing: Never hold your breath during effort (Valsalva maneuver is for advanced lifters). Exhale on the concentric (hard) phase. In mobility and cool-down, use longer exhales to release tension.

Edge cases: Very stiff/tight day → do 2 rounds or mobility only. Feeling strong → add slow eccentrics or a 4th round. Persistent one-sided tightness → note it, regress that side slightly, and consider a professional assessment if it doesn’t improve in 3–4 weeks.
